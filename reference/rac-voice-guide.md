# RAC Voice Guide

**Purpose:** Distillation of what RAC's published Intranet voice sounds like, drawn from real published examples. Used as reference for the `intranet-post` skill when generating titles, highlights, and any other text.

This is a guide for matching voice, not a style manual to enforce. RAC voice is recognisable but not formulaic. The goal is for skill-generated text to be indistinguishable from human-written RAC content — not to follow rigid rules.

---

## What RAC voice sounds like

**Warm.** Reads like it's written by someone who knows the people involved and likes them. Names get used. Photos are described affectionately ("She's a beauty!"). Achievements get celebrated, not just reported.

**Specific.** Real names, real places, real numbers. "Three Nhulunbuy stands" not "several stands". "Tripod" not "the trivia team". "Maggie Garawirrtja" not "an experienced mentor". Concrete beats abstract every time.

**Confident, not boastful.** RAC has plenty to be proud of and the voice owns it — but never overclaims. "Successful AGM" not "groundbreaking AGM". "Making headway" not "transformative progress". Australian understatement does the work.

**Casually professional.** Manager tabs are slightly more formal than Modal Stories, but never stiff. Trade vernacular is welcome ("Sparkies", "roughing in"). Internal references work fine ("Tripod" without explaining who Tripod is).

**Bilingual where it fits.** Yolŋu language appears naturally, often without translation, sometimes with a brief gloss. Never explained at length. Never used as decoration.

**Self-aware and occasionally self-deprecating.** "We're not perfect — Matt thought the song was 'These boots AINT made for walking'." The voice doesn't take itself too seriously.

---

## What RAC voice does NOT sound like

- ❌ **Corporate-speak:** "leverage", "synergies", "best practices", "strategic imperatives". Cut these without remorse.
- ❌ **Marketing hype:** "world-class", "industry-leading", "outstanding results". RAC is good. It doesn't need to puff its chest.
- ❌ **PR formality:** "RAC is pleased to announce that...". Just say what happened.
- ❌ **News-anchor neutrality:** "It is to be reported that...". The voice is human, not a press service.
- ❌ **LinkedIn motivational:** "Excited to share...", "humbled by the opportunity to...". Skip the throat-clearing entirely.
- ❌ **Sales pitch:** "Don't miss out!", "Click here to learn more!". The audience is staff, not prospects.
- ❌ **Listicle phrasings:** "5 things you need to know", "The top X of Y". Never.

## Core principles

### 1. Names beat descriptions

> "Lucky Band lays down vocal tracks" — names the band.
> "RAC Welcomes Adeline" — names the baby.
> "Bunggulwuy update" — names the project.

When the story has a person, place, project, group, or event with a name — use it. The skill should default to specificity wherever the submission text gives it.

### 2. Action beats abstraction

> "Tripod clean sweeps the Golf Club Trivia Night" — active verb.
> "Making headway at Bunggulwuy. Blockwork is almost complete." — concrete actions.
> "Sam and Hannah on the arrival of their beautiful daughter" — events, not states.

Verbs do the work. If you're using a noun phrase ("the launch of...", "the success of...") consider rewording with a verb.

### 3. Earn your superlatives

If something is genuinely great, say so. If it's not, don't pretend.

> "Successful AGM" — confident, not inflated.
> "Great official launch of Yanawal Kitchen" — the word "great" works because it earned it.
> Avoid "amazing", "incredible", "outstanding" unless the story really lives up to it.

### 4. Use Yolŋu language naturally

> "Gululu yuta yothu ngayiwuy Nhulunbuy - welcome new baby of Nhulunbuy"
> "Bunumbirr speaking with pride on behalf of the Rirratjingu community"

Yolŋu words appear in published RAC content as part of the voice, not as exotic insertions. The skill must:

- **Never "correct" Yolŋu words.** If a word looks unfamiliar, leave it exactly as written. It's almost certainly a name, place, or term that the submitter chose deliberately.
- **Never translate without invitation.** If the submitter wrote a Yolŋu phrase without a translation, don't add one. If they wrote one with a translation (as above), keep both.
- **Never italicise or otherwise mark Yolŋu language as foreign.** It's not foreign. It's part of the voice.

This applies to all non-English words in submissions, but is especially important for Yolŋu content given RAC's context.

### 5. Emoji as punctuation, not decoration

Emojis appear in real RAC highlights — but always at the end, almost always one (occasionally two), and always as a tonal stamp.

> "Tripod clean sweeps the Golf Club Trivia Night 💪"
> "Coaching from Maggie Garawirrtja powers the next track 🎤"

The skill can include emoji in highlights when generating them, with these rules:
- **Only at the end.** Never mid-sentence.
- **One emoji, occasionally two.** Never strings.
- **Match the feeling.** 💪 for wins. 💕 for family. 🎤 for music. 🏆 for achievements. ✌🏾 for community.
- **Skip if uncertain.** No emoji is better than the wrong emoji.
- **Never in titles.** Highlights only.

### 6. Length serves clarity

Titles: 2–6 words.
Highlights: 5–15 words mostly. Up to ~30 for multi-event recaps where every word earns its place.
Body: as long as the story needs. RAC has long bodies (the OPS Restructure, the Internal Comms Workshop) and short ones (the Team Photo). Both are valid.

If something feels long, it probably is. If something feels too brief, it's probably right — RAC voice errs toward concision.

## When the submission is messy

When you're cleaning a dictated story or generating from thin signal:

- **Preserve the submitter's voice.** If they say "us mob went down to..." keep it as written. Don't formalise to "the team travelled to...".
- **Preserve the submitter's intent.** If they're celebrating, the cleaned text should still celebrate. If they're reflecting, still reflect.
- **Preserve names and Yolŋu words exactly.** Even if they look unusual or you're uncertain of spelling.
- **Strip fillers and stutters only.** Not register, not warmth, not personality. (Detailed rules in `text-cleaning-rules.md`.)

---

## When the submission has no clear hero

Some submissions are vague — a few photos and "team day at the kitchen", or a board meeting with no obvious headline. The skill should:

- **Not fabricate.** Don't invent quotes, achievements, or details that aren't in the submission.
- **Not pad.** "It was a great day for everyone involved" is empty. Better to say less truly than more vaguely.
- **Lean on the photos.** Many RAC stories are essentially photo essays. The title and highlight just provide context.
- **Use neutral framings when content is thin.** "Team photo" is a valid highlight. "Site visit" works. "Team day at Yanawal Kitchen" if the location is known.

If you genuinely can't write something honest, write something short and accurate rather than something longer and embellished.

---

## When in doubt, ask: would Duane be happy to read this back?

The acid test for the skill's output:

> If Duane (or whoever's reviewing) read this on the live Intranet, would they think "yep, that sounds like us"? Or would they wince and rewrite it?

If the answer's the second one, the skill has missed the voice — even if the grammar is fine and the facts are right. Voice matters more than polish.

## The canonical use case: "Sam at the Quarry"

The story this whole system exists to capture:

> Sam (Business Manager) drives down to the Quarry mid-afternoon to help with a broken trailer. He's got a Marine and Heavy Machinery background — so it's not unusual for him to get his hands dirty. That's a thing around here. He's got fifteen minutes between meetings, takes three quick photos on his phone, dictates twenty seconds into the app:
>
> *"All in a day's work for the Bus.Dev manager helping out the guys to get the trailer back on the road. Job done!"*

That's it. That's the whole submission. And that's the kind of story that should land on the Intranet *the same afternoon* — not in next year's Annual Report.

What the skill should do with this submission:

- **Title (if blank):** Something like "All in a day's work" or "Trailer back on the road" — short, lifts a phrase from the submission.
- **Highlight (if blank):** The submitter's line is already perfect — *"All in a day's work for the Bus.Dev manager helping out the guys to get the trailer back on the road. Job done!"* — could become the highlight directly, or a tightened version of it. The skill should recognise when the submitter has already written something great and not "improve" it.
- **Cleaned text:** if dictated with fillers ("yeah look so I went down to the quarry um trailer was broken so I just got stuck in") clean those out, but preserve the tone — *"Went down to the quarry — trailer was broken so I just got stuck in. Got it sorted."*
- **AdminNote:** "Submitter's voice preserved verbatim. Title generated."

What the skill should NOT do:

- Inflate it. This is a 30-second story, not a "Manager Demonstrates Hands-On Leadership" feature.
- Add corporate framing. "Sam Hinton's hands-on approach exemplifies RAC values" is an immediate voice failure.
- Add emojis the submitter didn't ask for. (One at the end of the highlight is fine if the tone supports it — 💪 or 🔧 — but skip if uncertain.)
- Generate a long body. The submitter said twenty seconds. The published version should feel like twenty seconds.

This story is the test case for everything the skill needs to get right. If skill output for this kind of submission feels natural, the skill is working. If it feels inflated, polished, or AI-touched, the skill is over-reaching.

## Notes for the skill

When generating any text:

1. **Read the submission carefully first.** What's the energy? What's the hero (person, project, event)? What's the feeling?
2. **Match register to destination.** Modal Stories tab tolerates more casual energy. Manager tabs are slightly more formal but never stiff.
3. **Default to specific.** Names, places, numbers, verbs. Abstract is the failure mode.
4. **Default to short.** Cut before you add.
5. **Cross-check against examples.** Skim `example-titles.md` and `example-highlights.md` before generating. Would your output sit comfortably alongside them?
6. **If your output reads like a press release, redo it.** Voice failure.
7. **If your output reads like AI, redo it.** Especially: avoid "Discover", "Unleash", "Elevate", "Transform" as verbs. Avoid em-dashes in casual passages.
8. **When uncertain, lean on what's in the submission.** Don't guess at things you weren't told.

This is RAC voice. It belongs to the people who built it, and the skill's job is to honour it — not improve on it.
