# RAC Intranet Story Titles — Reference Examples

**Purpose:** Examples of real published RAC Intranet titles, used as reference for the `intranet-post` skill when generating titles for submissions where the submitter left the Title field blank.

**How to use this file:** When generating a title, read these examples first. Match the *pattern* — short, factual, sentence case, often action-oriented or naming what's central — not the topic. Don't pastiche specific phrasings; learn the shape.

---

## Modal Stories tab

### `Tripod does it Again!`
*Type:* Action / win
*Pattern:* Names the team ("Tripod"), states the achievement ("does it Again"), exclamation conveys energy. Casual, clearly internal-audience voice.
*Word count:* 4 words.

### `Australia Day Celebration`
*Type:* Event recap
*Pattern:* Plain factual naming — what it was. No hype needed; the event speaks for itself.
*Word count:* 3 words.

### `RAC Welcomes Adeline`
*Type:* Personal milestone (birth)
*Pattern:* Subject-verb-object. "RAC welcomes [name]" is a recurring shape worth knowing — works for births, new staff, new members. Warm without being gushy.
*Word count:* 3 words.

### `OPS Changes 2026`
*Type:* Operational announcement
*Pattern:* Department abbreviation + topic + year. Clear, scannable, signals "important update" without over-claiming.
*Word count:* 3 words.

### `Congratulations Graduates!`
*Type:* Celebration
*Pattern:* Single-word emotion + audience. Exclamation. No need to over-explain; the celebration *is* the point.
*Word count:* 2 words.

### `The Road Ahead`
*Type:* Strategic / event
*Pattern:* Borrowed event name. When something has a real name, use it. Don't invent a description for something that already has a title.
*Word count:* 3 words.

### `Yanawal Kitchen`
*Type:* Project completion
*Pattern:* Just the name of the thing. Sometimes the noun *is* the title — works when the audience already has context.
*Word count:* 2 words.

### `RAC Team`
*Type:* People / candid
*Pattern:* Generic but warm. Title carries no information; the photo and highlight do the work. This is fine — not every story needs a clever title.
*Word count:* 2 words.

## Manager-tier tabs (CEO / Business / Operations / Community / Safety)

### `Successful AGM held in November` *(CEO Messages)*
*Type:* CEO update / milestone
*Pattern:* Slightly more formal register than Modal. Past tense, factual, leads with the achievement ("Successful AGM"). Note: original had a typo ("Nomveber") — leave submitter's typos alone unless they're in *generated* content.
*Word count:* 5 words.

### `Internal Comms Workshop` *(Business Messages)*
*Type:* Operational summary
*Pattern:* What it was, no fanfare. Manager-tier titles often just name the activity — the audience expects detail in the body, not the title.
*Word count:* 3 words.

### `Bunggulwuy update` *(Operations Messages)*
*Type:* Project progress
*Pattern:* Project name + "update". Lowercase "update" is acceptable for ongoing/recurring topics where this is one in a series. Conveys: "you've heard of this; here's where it's at."
*Word count:* 2 words.

### `Lucky Band lays down vocal tracks` *(Community Messages)*
*Type:* Community recognition
*Pattern:* Subject + active verb + object. Specific, vivid verb ("lays down") rather than generic ("records"). Names the group. Reads like a news brief.
*Word count:* 6 words.

### `Happy National Safe Work Month` *(Safety Messages)*
*Type:* Awareness / campaign
*Pattern:* Greeting-style opener. Works because it sets a positive tone for what could otherwise feel preachy. Safety voice can be warm, doesn't have to be stern.
*Word count:* 5 words.

## Patterns at a glance

**Length:** 2–6 words. Most are 2–4. Keep it short.

**Case:** Sentence case (capitalise first word + proper nouns). Not Title Case. Not ALL CAPS. Not all-lowercase.

**Punctuation:** Exclamation marks are fine when there's genuine cause for celebration. Question marks rare. Periods almost never. Em-dashes never.

**Emojis:** Almost never in titles. Save them for highlights.

**Voice:** Direct, no padding. Avoid "How RAC did X" / "A look at Y" / "5 things about Z" — those are listicle/SEO patterns, not RAC's.

**Specificity:** When you can name the person, project, or event, do. "Lucky Band lays down vocal tracks" beats "Local band records music." "Yanawal Kitchen" beats "New facility opens."

**What to avoid:**
- Clickbait ("You won't believe what happened at the AGM!")
- Vague gerunds ("Building Better Together")
- Corporate adjectives ("Excellent results", "Outstanding achievement")
- Title Case capitalisation
- Long titles trying to summarise the whole story — that's the highlight's job

---

## Notes for the skill

When generating a title:

1. **First, check if there's a single concrete name** — a person, project, group, event. If yes, build the title around that name.
2. **If not, look for the action** — what happened? Use an active verb.
3. **If neither, fall back to event-naming** — what kind of thing is this story? ("Team photo", "Site update", "AGM recap").
4. **Constrain to 2–6 words.** If you can't get under 6, you're describing too much — the description belongs in the highlight or body.
5. **Sentence case. No emoji. No clickbait.**
6. **Stop and check:** would this look at home next to "Yanawal Kitchen" or "Tripod does it Again!" on the same page? If it looks like it came from a different publication, redo it.
